The Steam Engine

Before steam power, industries relied on people, animals, or rivers. In the 1760s, James Watt improved the engine’s efficiency by introducing a separate condenser, turning it into a powerful and dependable energy source. This single invention transformed mining, textiles, and transport, allowing factories to thrive in cities instead of being tied to water sources.

Steam trains and ships changed the scale of human connection. Trade grew, distances shrank, and time itself began to feel faster. The steam engine wasn’t just a machine—it was a new rhythm for life, marking the beginning of industrial modernity.

Even today, steam power lives on in turbines that generate electricity, proving that innovations from centuries ago still move the world forward.
